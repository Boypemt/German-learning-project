# How to learn German efficiently (evidence-based)

Research summary that drives this app's design. Sources at the bottom.

## The core principles

**1. Comprehensible input is the engine.** You acquire language by understanding messages slightly above your level (Krashen's i+1). Aim for material where you understand ~90–95%. Listening/reading at your level beats grammar drilling for long-term acquisition — one University of Maryland study found 30 min/day of comprehensible listening outperformed double the time on grammar exercises.

**2. Spaced repetition for vocabulary.** Review words just as they begin to fade. FSRS (used in this app) schedules each card individually and beats fixed schedules. Vocabulary size is the single biggest predictor of comprehension — the top ~5,000 word families cover ~95%+ of everyday text.

**3. Active recall > recognition.** Producing the word (typing it, saying it) builds stronger memory than multiple choice. This app's exercises always require production.

**4. Output practice early and often.** Speaking and writing force you to notice gaps. Speak at least 3×/week, write daily even if short. Errors are data, not failure.

**5. Consistency beats intensity.** 45–60 min every day outperforms 5 hours on Sunday. Streaks matter — the dashboard tracks yours.

**6. Frequency-ordered learning.** Learn the most common words and structures first (this app's decks are frequency-ordered from the Leipzig/OpenSubtitles corpora).

## Realistic timeline to C1

FSI and Goethe-Institut estimates converge on roughly **750–900 study hours** for an English speaker to reach C1 German.

| Daily time | Time to C1 |
|---|---|
| 1 h/day | ~2.5 years |
| 2 h/day | ~14 months |
| 3 h/day | ~10 months |

Level milestones (cumulative hours, approximate): A1 ~80–100, A2 ~200, B1 ~350–400, B2 ~600, C1 ~750–900.

## The daily routine this app supports

| Block | Time | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| Vocab | 15–20 min | Clear FSRS due queue + 10–20 new cards |
| Listening | 15 min | Dictation drills, then native podcasts/YouTube at your level |
| Grammar | 10 min | One topic + exercises (only until B1–B2; after that, learn grammar from input) |
| Speaking | 10 min | Shadowing + pronunciation drills in the app |
| Writing | 10 min | One prompt; check with Claude or DeepL Write |

Plus **free immersion**: German podcasts, Netflix with German subs, Easy German on YouTube — as much as life allows. From B1 on, add real conversation (Tandem partners, r/German Discords, or a local Stammtisch) — an app alone plateaus around B1 without real interaction.

## Level-by-level focus

- **A1–A2 (months 0–4):** Top 1,000–2,000 words, present/perfect tense, word order, cases (nominative/accusative). Massive listening to slow German (Nicos Weg A1/A2 is free).
- **B1 (months 4–8):** Top 3,000–4,000 words, all tenses, dative/genitive, subordinate clauses. Start real conversations. Nicos Weg B1, Easy German podcast.
- **B2 (months 8–14):** Passive, Konjunktiv II, adjective endings automatic. Native content becomes main input. Write 150+ words/day. Work-domain vocabulary.
- **C1 (months 14–20):** Nominalization, Konjunktiv I, idiomatic range, register (formal emails vs. Umgangssprache). Prepare specifically for the exam format you'll take (Goethe C1 / telc C1 / TestDaF — TestDaF and telc C1 Hochschule matter for university; Goethe/telc C1 for work). Practice exams are the highest-leverage activity in the last 2 months.

## Pronunciation (do this from day 1)

German pronunciation is regular — learn the letter-to-sound rules once and everything is readable. Priorities: ü/ö vowels, ch (ich vs. ach), r, final devoicing (Tag → "Tak"), word stress. Method: **shadowing** (repeat immediately after native audio, imitating rhythm) + minimal pairs. The Speaking page implements listen → record → compare.
